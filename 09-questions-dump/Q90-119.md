**Q90: A RAG application has context_recall = 0.94, faithfulness = 0.61, and answer_relevance = 0.89 on an offline evaluation run. A Generative AI Engineer is triaging which component to fix first. What does this metric combination MOST likely indicate, and which component should be improved?**

O: a) Retrieval is missing relevant documents; increase top-k or improve the embedding model
   b) The retrieval is working well (high recall), and answers are addressing the right question (high relevance), but the generation model is frequently producing claims not supported by the retrieved context (low faithfulness) — the prompt instructions, generation temperature, or grounding constraints should be adjusted
   c) The vector index is corrupted; rebuild the index from scratch
   d) The evaluation judges are misconfigured; faithfulness scores below 0.8 always indicate a judge failure

A: **B**. Context_recall = 0.94 means retrieval is finding nearly all relevant chunks — the retrieval layer is healthy. Answer_relevance = 0.89 means the answers address what the user asked. Faithfulness = 0.61 is the outlier: it reveals that despite good retrieval and question-targeting, the LLM is generating claims that are not supported by the retrieved context — a hallucination/grounding failure in the generation step. The fix is generation-side: stricter grounding instructions in the system prompt, lower temperature, or explicit instruction to cite context only. Option A is wrong — recall is high. Options C and D are unsupported by the data.

**Q91: A Generative AI Engineer has two chunking strategies that score nearly identically on recall@10, but Strategy A has noticeably higher NDCG@10 than Strategy B. What does this difference most likely indicate?**

O: a) Strategy A retrieves relevant documents more often than Strategy B.
   b) Strategy A tends to place the most relevant chunks higher in the ranked results than Strategy B, even though both retrieve similar sets of relevant chunks overall.
   c) Strategy A uses a smaller embedding model.
   d) NDCG and recall must always be identical if chunk counts are equal.

A: **B**. Since recall@10 is nearly equal (similar sets of relevant items are retrieved), a higher NDCG@10 for Strategy A indicates better rank ordering — the relevant chunks appear earlier in the list — not a difference in whether they were retrieved at all.

**Q92: A team self-hosts an open-source LLM with a 4K token context window behind a Databricks Model Serving endpoint. After switching their RAG pipeline to retrieve the top 8 chunks (each ~600 tokens) instead of top 4, latency increases sharply and some requests fail. O: a) Increase the model's temperature to reduce output length.
   b) Add a re-ranking step to select the most relevant subset (e.g., top 4) of the 8 retrieved chunks to actually pass into the generation prompt, keeping broader initial retrieval for recall while controlling final context size.
   c) Switch the embedding model to one with a larger context length.
   d) Remove the vector database entirely.

A: **B**. Re-ranking lets the system retrieve broadly (8 chunks, good recall) but forward only the most relevant subset to the context-limited generation model, resolving the token overflow without sacrificing retrieval quality or changing the model.

**Q99: A Generative AI Engineer's LLM-as-a-judge for faithfulness gives inconsistent scores (sometimes rating the same answer/context pair differently across runs). What is the MOST likely cause, and what is a reasonable mitigation?**

O: a) The judge is fundamentally broken and LLM-as-a-judge should never be used.
   b) Nondeterminism in the judge LLM's generation (e.g., from temperature > 0) can cause score variance; using a low or zero temperature for the judge model and/or aggregating scores across multiple runs can improve consistency.
   c) The application's retrieval component must be at fault, not the judge.
   d) Faithfulness cannot be measured by an LLM judge under any configuration.

A: **B**. LLM judges are themselves LLMs subject to generation variance; setting the judge's temperature low (or to zero) and/or averaging over multiple evaluation runs are standard mitigations for score inconsistency, rather than concluding the technique itself is invalid.
