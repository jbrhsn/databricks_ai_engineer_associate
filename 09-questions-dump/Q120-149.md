**Q126: A Generative AI Engineer is evaluating retrieval performance and wants a single metric that reflects the proportion of retrieved chunks that are actually relevant (as opposed to whether all relevant chunks were found). Which metric fits this description?**

O: a) Context precision
   b) Context recall
   c) NDCG
   d) Perplexity

A: **A**. Context precision specifically measures what proportion of the retrieved chunks are relevant, focusing on the "noise" in what's returned, as distinct from recall (which measures whether all relevant chunks were found).

**Q127: A Generative AI Engineer added a cross-encoder re-ranker to a RAG pipeline. After the change, context_recall@10 dropped slightly (from 0.88 to 0.84) but answer_relevance and faithfulness both improved significantly. How should the engineer interpret this result?**

O: a) The re-ranker is broken — any drop in recall is a defect that must be fixed before the improvement in answer quality can be accepted
   b) This is the expected precision-recall trade-off of re-ranking: the re-ranker may occasionally demote or drop a relevant chunk (slightly reducing recall) while pushing the most pertinent chunks to the top-k passed to the LLM, which improves generation quality. If the downstream quality gains outweigh the small recall drop for the use case, this is an acceptable and successful outcome
   c) Re-ranking can only improve recall, never reduce it; the metric drop must be caused by an unrelated index change
   d) The faithfulness and relevance improvements are coincidental and should be ignored until recall is restored to 0.88

A: **B**. Re-ranking is a precision optimisation: it selects the highest-quality subset from the initial retrieved candidates to pass to the LLM. This inherently means some relevant (but lower-ranked) chunks may be excluded, which can slightly reduce recall. The observed outcome — marginal recall decrease, significant generation quality improvement — is the characteristic footprint of a well-functioning re-ranker. The engineer should decide based on the use case whether the quality gain justifies the recall trade-off. Option A incorrectly treats any recall drop as a defect. Options C and D are factually wrong.
